Someone once said that to understand what frightens you, you must first face it. For me, that fear was public speaking.

Communication is one of the most vital skills in life. How you speak is a mirror that reflects who you are on the inside. I learned this the hard way, across five very different phases of my life.

## Phase 1: The Shy Kid

I was an extremely shy child. So withdrawn, in fact, that telling a neighbour she resembled Nelson Mandela was probably my boldest move for years. The idea of speaking in front of people was terrifying.

## Phase 2: The College Turning Point

College changed everything. My chemistry professor, Dr. Ronald Nazareth, was one of the finest communicators I had ever seen. His knowledge and clarity of speech were genuinely inspiring. At my college farewell, something clicked — I volunteered to speak publicly for the first time. It was terrifying. It was also foundational.

## Phase 3: Finding My Voice

Through friendships and years of observing people, I became more vocal. I discovered I had a talent for mimicry and impressions, and started performing at various forums. When the 2008 recession delayed my corporate career, I ended up teaching engineering students — which, in hindsight, was one of the best things that could have happened to my confidence as a speaker.

## Phase 4: The Wedding Toast

My friend Alan asked me to deliver a toast at his wedding. Others were sceptical. I was nervous. But the speech went remarkably well — even the bride's father, who had doubted me, came around. That moment told me something.

## Phase 5: Toastmasters

I resisted joining Toastmasters for a while. My colleague Mathew kept pushing. Eventually I gave in — and I am glad I did. It gave structure and discipline to abilities I had always had but never properly developed. I went on to win a humorous speech contest.

Next stop: stand-up comedy. Someday.

Whatever your fear is — pursue it anyway. The worst that can happen is a story worth telling.
